# I Kept Three Percent

I forked GSD and deleted almost all of it. Seventy-one skills down to twenty-two, thirty-four agents down to seven, and the number that actually tells the story, one-point-one million words of documentation down to thirty-three thousand. Cadence is about three percent of what I started with. People hear that and assume I gutted something good. I did the opposite. I went in after the good part, and the good part was buried.

Because GSD gets the hard thing right. The loop is sound, discuss and plan and execute and verify, one atomic commit at a time, a plan you can check before you build against it. That is a real methodology and I kept every bone of it. Whoever laid that out had done the work of thinking about how software actually gets made, and I am not too proud to say I took it.

Then it feels like they never handed it to an engineer. It reads like a project manager wrote the design and an agile coach filled in around it, and nobody in the room ever said no. Seventy-one skills, and seventy-one separate commands stacked on top of them, and forty-six "capabilities" underneath that, a debugger agent that runs fifty kilobytes by itself, a context file that runs two hundred twenty-seven. A skill to sketch. A skill to graphify. A skill for forensics. Namespace variants of workflows shelved next to the workflows they vary. That is not a product with a vision, that is a shelf where every idea anyone ever had got set down and left, and I have watched software rot exactly this way before.

Every feature you ship is a thing that can fail, every path through the code is a path a mistake can walk down, and the surface you never build is the only surface that never betrays you. A tool that cannot say no to a feature cannot say yes to being correct.

Cadence says no out loud and on purpose. Because it only runs on Claude Code the whole multi-host shim went in the bin, the .kilo and .opencode and vscode folders and the forty lines of locator glue that were pasted into the top of every workflow. I am one person, so the team tooling and the multi-author machinery went with it. The AI-product track, the UI auditors, the research fan-outs, the memory-palace curators, all gone, because a working developer already has a memory and a graph and does not need the tool to sprout its own.

What stayed, I tuned like it costs money, because it does. Tokens are a budget, every dispatch is a bet on whether the answer is worth what it burns. Cadence sends a heavy model to the judgment and a cheap one to the rote work and the same allowance goes further. The gates are real gates, not suggestions. A plan gets checked before you build against it. A diff hits a review before it lands. A commit onto a protected branch stops and asks. I spent a full day this week measuring cache reads on documentation instead of writing any, which is either discipline or a sickness, and the measurement talked me out of half the changes I had my heart set on, which is the entire reason you measure.

And it is mine, all the way down. GSD ships its own machinery to chase upstream, sync the skills, reapply the patches, merge three ways, and at three percent retained with every survivor rewritten there is nothing left to merge against, so I stopped pretending there was. The repo is the truth. Upstream will have good ideas and I will take them one at a time, by hand, on the day I decide they have earned it, not on the day a pull turns red. I have shipped through enough of other people's gates to know that the day the rules change, or the mood does, everyone standing on that gate with you is the one who falls through.

GSD kept asking what else it could hold. Cadence asks what it can put down and still deliver. I did not want a lighter GSD, I wanted the loop, held in one hand, by someone willing to say no. So I stopped trimming theirs and built my own.
